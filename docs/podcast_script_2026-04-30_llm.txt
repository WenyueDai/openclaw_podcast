The biggest shift happening right now is that we can finally design protein binders from scratch instead of fishing for them. A paper on AI-driven de novo binder design shows the field moving away from decades of screening-based discovery — no more relying on immunization or phage display to find what binds. Instead, machine learning models can generate novel antibodies, monobodies, and designed ankyrin repeat proteins against targets we've chosen ahead of time. For someone running a protein design project, this means you no longer need a massive library or an animal immune system. You need a good model and a clear structural hypothesis.

But here's what might surprise you: bigger models aren't automatically better. A benchmark across 22 molecular property and activity endpoints — spanning public ADMET and Tox21 datasets plus internal anti-infective data — found that large generalist language models didn't consistently beat compact, specialized graph neural networks or traditional cheminformatics models. The 167,000 compounds tested showed that task-specific training matters more than parameter count. If you've been chasing the latest billion-parameter foundation model for your property predictions, this is a signal to reconsider. Sometimes a well-tuned smaller model gives you the same accuracy with a fraction of the compute.

On the chromatin biology side, a new method called SAMOSA is changing how we see nucleosomes at the single-molecule level. Using methyltransferase footprinting combined with PacBio SMRT sequencing, researchers can now map nucleosome positioning and distortion across individual chromatin fibers in mouse embryonic stem cells, primary hepatocytes, and various genetic perturbations. The key insight is that nucleosome distortion isn't rare — it's pervasive and programmed. This matters for protein designers because nucleosome accessibility determines whether transcription factors or engineered DNA-binding proteins can actually reach their targets in vivo. If you're designing chromatin-binding domains, knowing that nucleosomes are constantly deformed rather than static obstacles changes how you should think about target accessibility.

Two additional papers connect chromatin regulation to disease. HP1, traditionally known as a heterochromatin marker, directly interacts with the nuclear RNA exosome to affect transcriptional programs in mouse embryonic liver cells — an unexpected bridge between chromatin architecture and RNA decay. Meanwhile, the HIRA-SETDB1-H3K9me3 axis regulates chromatin reorganization in chronic myeloid leukemia, with HIRA expression significantly elevated in patient samples compared to healthy controls. These suggest that chromatin regulatory proteins aren't just passive structural elements; they're active nodes in disease-specific networks that could become design targets themselves.

The SAMOSA method for mapping chromatin accessibility at single-molecule resolution is genuinely clever, and I want to dig into why a naive approach would have failed. The core innovation here is using EcoGII methyltransferase to footprint accessible DNA in situ — you incubate nuclei with the enzyme and it adds methyl groups to exposed adenines, then you sequence long fragments with PacBio and read the methylation pattern directly. The reason this matters is that traditional ATAC-seq gives you population averages and loses phase information across nucleosomes, while short-read methods can't reliably connect accessibility patterns that span more than a few hundred bases. With SAMOSA, you get kilobase-scale reads that show you exactly how nucleosome positioning, nucleosome-free regions, and higher-order structures coexist on the same DNA molecule. That's the specific question they wanted to answer: can we see the full chromatin architecture at single-molecule resolution?

A naive MTase approach would have failed because you can't just grind up nuclei and expect clean signal — the authors used live nuclei or carefully permeabilized cells, titrated EcoGII to 1 micromolar with S-adenosylmethionine, and then needed a quality filter. That's where the MDR threshold comes in: they required mononucleosome-to-dinucleosome ratio greater than 10, which sounds arbitrary but it's actually a stringent filter that excludes fragments where MNase didn't cleanly release mononucleosomes. They validated this across six different cell lines including primary hepatocytes, and did Western blots to confirm actual protein depletion with their dTAG systems — so the perturbation biology is grounded.

For the model scaling paper, the cleverness is in the benchmark design itself. They didn't just compare one large model against one small model — they tested 22 different endpoints spanning ADMET, Tox21, and internal anti-infective data, totaling over 167,000 compounds. The adversarial test was asking: does a generalist foundation model actually beat a specialized graph neural network trained specifically for, say, CYP inhibition? The answer was often no, and that's the interesting result. But the methodological point is that they designed the benchmark to be hard for large models — including internal datasets that wouldn't be in any pretraining corpus — which is exactly the kind of stress test that could have failed but didn't.
