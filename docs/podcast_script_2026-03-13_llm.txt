Some experimental studies suggested that knotted proteins are more thermally stable, while computational studies often found no such effect. This contradiction has puzzled the field, but a new study on the protein YibK offers a compelling resolution.

The researchers used extensive Monte Carlo simulations with a simple C-alpha model and Go potential to examine YibK in both its knotted and unknotted forms. Their key finding was striking: the melting temperature, or Tm, showed no dependence on whether the protein was knotted or unknotted. This directly contradicts some experimental observations but aligns with many computational results.

So why the discrepancy? The answer lies in the timescales of the processes involved. In deeply knotted proteins like YibK, the timescales for unknotting and unfolding are dramatically different. Instead, the experiment captures a non-equilibrium apparent Tm, not the true equilibrium property.

This insight is crucial. It suggests that experimental measurements of Tm for knotted proteins may not reflect their true thermal stability but rather a kinetic bottleneck. The study resolves a long-standing conflict in the literature by showing that the apparent effect of knots on thermal stability is likely an artifact of experimental timescales, not a fundamental property of the protein's topology.

This work has significant implications for interpreting experimental data and understanding protein folding landscapes. It highlights the importance of considering kinetic factors when studying complex biomolecular systems and suggests that future experiments may need to account for these timescale separations to accurately assess the role of topology in protein stability.

Common random numbers are a standard variance reduction technique in agent-based models, where the same random inputs are reused across intervention scenarios to produce paired counterfactual simulations. The idea is simple: if two simulations share the same random draws, their differences should isolate the causal effect of the intervention, reducing variance compared to independent runs.

But there's a critical flaw in how CRNs are typically implemented. Most agent-based models use stateful pseudorandom number generators, where each call to the PRNG advances its internal state. When an intervention changes the simulation's execution path—say, by altering which agents interact or when events occur—the sequence of random draws shifts. A random number that once determined an agent's movement might now determine something else entirely. This breaks the assumed stable mapping between random draws and modeled events, making the paired simulations causally invalid.

The problem is that standard CRN implementations assume a fixed correspondence between random draws and events, but stateful PRNGs don't guarantee this when the simulation path changes. The variance reduction comes at the cost of biased causal estimates.

The solution proposed here is event-keyed hashing. Instead of using a stateful PRNG, each modeled event is assigned a unique key—derived from the event's type, location, and time. A pseudorandom number for that event is then generated by hashing its key with a shared random seed. Because the key is deterministic and tied to the event's identity, the same event always produces the same random number, regardless of when or how often it's sampled. This eliminates the dependency on execution order and ensures that interventions only affect events they directly modify.

The approach is implemented in a Python package called crn4abm, which provides drop-in replacements for NumPy's random functions. Benchmarks show statistically significant variance reduction in several case studies, with performance overhead typically under 25% compared to standard CRNs. The method restores both the variance benefits and causal validity of common random numbers in agent-based modeling.

The problem is that cluster inference depends critically on choosing a primary threshold—too high and you miss real effects; too low and you drown in noise.

Threshold-free cluster enhancement, or TFCE, was introduced to sidestep this dilemma. Instead of picking one threshold, TFCE integrates evidence across all possible thresholds, combining both cluster extent and voxel height into a single enhancement score. The catch? Computing TFCE p-values requires permutation testing—randomly shuffling group labels thousands of times to build a null distribution. For high-resolution brain images, this can take hours or even days.

A recent pre-print proposes a hybrid solution called eTFCE-GRF that aims to keep TFCE's sensitivity while delivering permutation-free speed. The method works by first applying TFCE to the statistical map, then retrieving the exact cluster extent at the threshold that produced each TFCE score. Critically, it assigns analytical p-values to these retrieved extents using Gaussian random field theory—the same framework that powers tools like FSL's randomise. Because GRF theory provides closed-form p-value calculations, the entire inference pipeline runs in seconds rather than hours.

The authors validate the approach on simulated and real VBM data, showing that eTFCE-GRF recovers clusters nearly identical to those found by full permutation TFCE, but with orders-of-magnitude faster computation. They also demonstrate that the retrieved extents match the true extent at the maximizing threshold, ensuring no loss of spatial precision. The method is implemented in a lightweight Python package and is positioned as a drop-in replacement for existing TFCE workflows.

If the results hold up, eTFCE-GRF could become a new standard for VBM studies, offering the sensitivity of threshold-free inference without the computational bottleneck that has limited its use in large-scale or clinical applications.

Absolute Binding Free Energy methods are among the most accurate computational techniques for predicting how strongly a small molecule binds to a protein.
