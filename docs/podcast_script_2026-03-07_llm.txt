Chalkiness—a quality defect that reduces both appearance and market value—arises when starch synthesis is disrupted during grain filling. Researchers found that DAAT1 helps maintain ER homeostasis under stress conditions, preventing chalk formation. This discovery sheds light on D-amino acid metabolism in plants, a field previously dominated by studies in bacteria and animals. The findings open new avenues for breeding high-quality rice varieties with improved grain appearance and cooking properties.

Enzyme kinetic analysis has evolved from traditional steady-state approximations to modern computational methods, according to a review in Biochemistry. The paper traces how 20th-century approaches relied on simplified rate equations and analytical integration of differential equations for transient kinetics. Today, researchers use numerical integration of rate equations with direct fitting to experimental data, enabling more accurate modeling of complex enzyme mechanisms. This computational shift allows scientists to handle multiple substrates, inhibitors, and allosteric effects that were previously intractable. The review highlights how these advances have transformed our ability to understand enzyme behavior, moving beyond the limitations of analytical solutions to embrace the power of modern computing in biochemical research.

A new preprint from bioRxiv reveals that diabetic endothelial dysfunction is driven by rewiring of 3D enhancer-promoter interactions in the genome. Researchers generated a high-resolution 3D epigenomic atlas of diabetic endothelium, uncovering how chromatin architecture is altered in diabetes. The study identifies key regulators involved in these structural changes, suggesting that disrupted 3D genome organization plays a central role in diabetic vascular complications. This work provides the first comprehensive view of how 3D enhancer-promoter interactomes are rewired in diabetic endothelium, offering new insights into the molecular mechanisms underlying diabetic vascular disease and potential therapeutic targets.

INTENSE is a new open-source framework designed to detect and disentangle how neurons encode behavior from calcium imaging data. The core innovation is using information theory—specifically mutual information—to quantify the association between continuous calcium fluorescence signals and behavioral variables, while rigorously controlling for the temporal autocorrelation inherent in neural recordings.

The method works by first computing mutual information between neural activity and each behavioral variable. To avoid false positives from temporal structure, it uses circular-shift permutation testing, which preserves the autocorrelation of the data while breaking any real association. This is critical because standard shuffling methods can inflate false discovery rates when neural signals are autocorrelated. INTENSE also optimizes temporal delays to account for calcium indicator kinetics, allowing it to detect both prospective encoding (where neural activity predicts upcoming behavior) and retrospective encoding (where activity reflects past events).

A key advance is the use of conditional mutual information to disentangle "mixed selectivity"—genuine multi-variable encoding—from associations that arise simply because behavioral variables are correlated. For example, if an animal's position and head direction are correlated, a neuron might appear to encode both, but INTENSE can determine whether it truly encodes both independently or just one with the other riding along.

Validation on synthetic data showed INTENSE robustly detects selectivity across a range of signal-to-noise ratios, while methods without proper temporal controls performed unreliably. Applied to real hippocampal miniscope recordings from mice exploring an open field, INTENSE identified neurons with robust selectivity to multiple behavioral variables—place, head direction, object interaction, and locomotion—and refined estimates of mixed selectivity by distinguishing neurons that redundantly encode correlated behaviors from those that genuinely encode multiple independent variables.

This framework enables high-throughput, principled mapping of neuronal selectivity from large-scale calcium imaging datasets, bridging the gap between population recordings and circuit-level hypotheses about how neurons encode environmental features and behavior. Diffusion MRI has long been used to probe tissue microstructure, but most methods either assume rigid barriers or only estimate bulk parameters without recovering actual interfaces. Spinverse takes a different approach: it reconstructs explicit microstructural boundaries directly from dMRI data by treating permeability as a learnable property on a fixed tetrahedral mesh.

The key innovation is that Spinverse does not predefine the topology of tissue barriers. Instead, it starts with a uniform tetrahedral grid and assigns each interior face a permeability parameter. During optimization, faces with low permeability naturally emerge as diffusion barriers, effectively reconstructing the microstructure without prior assumptions about its geometry.

The method works by simulating diffusion through the mesh using a differentiable Bloch-Torrey PDE solver. This allows backpropagation through the physics simulation, so the permeability parameters can be optimized to minimize the difference between simulated and measured dMRI signals. Because the forward model is differentiable, the entire pipeline—from microstructure to signal—can be inverted in a single optimization.

This is a shift from traditional approaches that either model tissue as a continuum or require manual specification of barriers. Here, the barriers self-organize from the data, guided by the physics of diffusion. The result is a permeability-aware reconstruction that can recover explicit interfaces such as cell membranes or axonal boundaries directly from the dMRI measurements.

It also does not specify the computational cost or memory requirements of the differentiable PDE solver. However, the core contribution is clear: Spinverse enables microstructure reconstruction from dMRI by learning permeability on a fixed grid, letting diffusion barriers emerge from the data rather than being imposed a priori.
