The way we think about protein structure prediction is quietly shifting, and today's papers capture that momentum in several different directions at once.

The most tangible shift is in how we approach higher-order protein complexes. For decades, structural biology has mostly stopped at pairwise interactions — you get a dimer and call it a day. But Alphafuser shows you can actually go from binary interaction data to trimeric and tetrameric structures in a computationally tractable way. They tested this on a chloroplast RNA-binding protein interactome with 40 members, which mathematically yields over a trillion possible combinations. Their pipeline uses the ipTM score from AlphaFold to iteratively prune unlikely complexes, so you only compute the ones that matter. They validated experimentally — pull-down assays confirmed interactions even with ipTM scores as low as 0.2, which is remarkably permissive and suggests the predictions are capturing real biology, not just high-confidence obvious pairs.

A second shift is in conformational diversity. AlphaFold gives you one structure per sequence, but proteins function by moving between multiple states. ConforFold addresses this by integrating secondary-structure sampling directly into the prediction pipeline, going beyond what MSA subsampling alone can recover. This matters for anyone designing against a dynamic target — you need to know what the alternative states look like, not just the ground state.

A third shift is more subtle but potentially huge: protein language models can now detect ancient duplication events that sequence alignment methods miss. pLM-Repeat uses the contextual representations from models like ESM to find repeats even when they've accumulated substantial sequence divergence. This changes how you might think about evolutionary analysis — the signal is there in the representations even when it's invisible to traditional BLAST or HMM approaches.

On the drug discovery side, a new graph neural network with group lasso regularization demonstrates that you can predict compound-protein affinity while also explaining which molecular groups drive the binding. That's the Holy Grail for explainable AI in this space — not just a prediction, but the reasoning behind it.

And in industry news, the FDA's recent action on Tavneos signals that regulatory scrutiny on approved drugs isn't easing, which makes the case for better computational prediction and validation only stronger.

The papers we have been discussing sit at the intersection of several deep currents in biochemistry that run far deeper than any single method or dataset. When pLM-Repeat uses protein language models to detect ancient duplication events, it is really grappling with a problem that dates back to Susumu Ohno's 1970 book "Evolution by Gene Duplication" — the idea that the primary engine of biological novelty is copy-and-paste at the genetic level, followed by divergence. What makes this detection hard is fundamentally an information theory problem: as mutations accumulate, the signal of common ancestry degrades like a radio transmission through static, and the question becomes how much signal remains extractable from sequence alone. Language models trained on millions of proteins have effectively learned to hear that signal even when it has become nearly inaudible to conventional methods, which is remarkable because it means evolutionary information is more robustly encoded in protein sequences than we often assume.

ConforFold's approach to recovering alternative conformations echoes the energy landscape framework that Bryngelson, Wolynes, and collaborators developed throughout the 1990s — the notion that proteins do not adopt a single structure but navigate a funnel-shaped landscape toward the native state, with multiple minima accessible under different conditions. The challenge has always been that current structure predictors, including AlphaFold, are optimized to find the lowest-energy basin, not to map the full terrain. ConforFold's integration of secondary-structure sampling is a pragmatic workaround: by perturbing one of the key determinants of folding early in the process, they effectively shake the system out of its default minimum and let it settle into neighboring basins. This is conceptually similar to temperature-accelerated molecular dynamics, but implemented at the level of the neural network's internal representations.

Alphafuser addresses a problem that is fundamentally statistical mechanical: the combinatorial explosion of possible multi-protein assemblies. A 40-member interactome yields over a trillion possible combinations, which is not a parameter-fitting problem but a sampling problem — how to navigate an exponentially large configuration space without exhaustive enumeration. Their use of ipTM scores as a pruning metric is essentially a mean-field approximation: they assume that the probability of a higher-order complex can be approximated from the pairwise interaction strengths, which works reasonably well for cooperative assemblies but may miss cases where weak individual interfaces become stabilized through allosteric contributions from a third partner. This limitation has been discussed in the macromolecular assembly literature for decades, going back to early work on the thermodynamics of cooperativity in protein complexes.
