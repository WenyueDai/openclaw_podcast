Welcome to Colorful Biology, where we explore the living world through the lens of data, models, and a dash of curiosity. Today’s deep dive, drawn from a recent Quanta Magazine feature, looks at a surprisingly “ghostly” problem in climate science: clouds. The headline claim is that uncertainty about how climate models treat clouds accounts for more than half of the spread in future warming projections. In other words, clouds are the biggest wildcard in our forecasts of how hot the planet will get.

What do we actually know? The article tells us that a 2–3 percent error in cloud‑cover fraction can shift projected warming by several degrees Celsius. That is a concrete figure, and it underscores why clouds dominate the error budget. Even the 2022 Department of Energy model run on the Frontier supercomputer at roughly three‑kilometer resolution still cannot resolve individual clouds. To capture low‑level clouds directly would require about one hundred billion times the compute power we currently have—a scale that is, for now, purely theoretical.

Two research teams are tackling this challenge with artificial‑intelligence strategies. The first, led by climate scientist Schneider, is building a next‑generation model called CLIMA. CLIMA couples the traditional Navier‑Stokes equations—those governing fluid flow in the atmosphere—with machine‑learning algorithms that learn to pick the optimal cloud‑parameter values. The AI is trained on a library of high‑resolution cloud simulations known as large‑eddy simulations, or LES. LES are detailed, physics‑based models that resolve turbulent eddies in clouds, giving us a “microscope” view of cloud behavior. Historically, only a few dozen high‑quality LES existed, which was insufficient for robust AI training. Schneider has now partnered with Google to expand this dataset, creating a pipeline where the AI can tune cloud parameters more accurately.

The second approach, championed by Bretherton, takes a different route. Rather than embedding AI within a physics‑heavy model, Bretherton’s team develops tools that learn directly from real‑world atmospheric observations and a limited set of LES data. In this scenario, the AI bypasses most of the governing equations, aiming to forecast climate states with minimal explicit physics. Both groups rely on massive computational resources—DOE’s Frontier supercomputer and Google’s AI infrastructure—to generate and process the training data.

Why does this matter? Climate projections guide mitigation and adaptation policies worldwide. If we can shrink the cloud‑related uncertainty, the current projected warming range of two to six degrees Celsius could collapse into a narrower, more manageable band. Faster, more reliable models are essential because, as the article notes, “climate is changing fast,” and waiting for a perfect physics‑only model could mean missing the window for effective action.

Now, let’s be clear about the unknowns. The available text does not provide details on how well the AI‑augmented models perform on independent test cases, nor does it give specific metrics of improvement over existing models. Data scarcity remains a major limitation: real‑world cloud observations and LES are limited, and the AI’s performance hinges on the breadth and quality of this training set. Computational cost is another hurdle; generating enough LES to cover the full spectrum of atmospheric conditions is still extremely expensive, even with the most powerful supercomputers. Finally, models that “barely rely on physics equations” may capture statistical patterns but could miss causal mechanisms, leading to unpredictable behavior when applied outside the training regime. In other words, we have a promising path, but the road is still under construction.

To sum up, clouds are the ghostly specters haunting climate projections, and AI is being invited in as a kind of scientific exorcist. Whether the AI can reliably predict the shape of those specters remains an open question, but the effort is a vivid illustration of how computational tools are reshaping our understanding of the Earth system. Stay tuned for more updates, and keep your curiosity cloud‑ready.

RNF123, an E3 ubiquitin ligase, adds a specific tag called K63‑linked ubiquitin chains to NEK7, a kinase that normally helps build the inflammasome.  “Unanchored” means the ubiquitin chains are not attached to a substrate protein, acting more like a free signal.  This tagging blocks the NLRP3 complex from forming, offering a potential therapeutic angle for Crohn’s disease and other inflammatory disorders.  The abstract does not reveal experimental models or quantitative effects, so the strength of inhibition is still uncertain, but the mechanistic insight is striking.

Age‑related macular degeneration (AMD) gets a new molecular antagonist in DAPL1, according to a February 2026 PNAS report. It does so by blocking GRP75‑mediated mitochondria‑associated endoplasmic reticulum membranes (MAMs), the contact sites that coordinate calcium signaling and metabolic crosstalk between mitochondria and the ER. By preserving RPE integrity, DAPL1 may slow the cascade that leads to vision loss. The snippet does not reveal the experimental design (e.g., mouse models, dosing) or the magnitude of protection, so the exact therapeutic promise remains tentative, but the findings spotlight a novel target in the fight against AMD.

Robust input disentanglement through dendritic calcium‑mediated action potentials – PNAS presents a computational study showing that single neurons can untangle overlapping streams of sensory input by generating calcium spikes in their dendrites. The model demonstrates that dendritic calcium‑mediated action potentials act as local amplifiers, allowing distinct input patterns to be separated without requiring large‑scale network re‑wiring. While the simulations are compelling, the authors note that experimental confirmation in living tissue is still pending, so the biological relevance is not yet fully established. Lipoprotein diffusion in dense yolk plasma is governed by softness, hydrodynamics, and caging: Insights from MHz‑XPCS – PNAS.  Egg yolk is a remarkably crowded fluid that stores the lipids needed for a developing embryo, yet the low‑density lipoproteins (LDLs) that ferry those lipids still have to move.  In this study the authors turned to megahertz X‑ray photon correlation spectroscopy (MHz‑XPCS), a technique that flashes X‑rays at million‑times‑per‑second rates and watches the speckle pattern wobble to infer particle motion.  Their measurements reveal that three intertwined factors dominate LDL diffusion: the softness of the surrounding protein matrix, the fluid’s hydrodynamic flow, and “caging,” a phenomenon where a particle is temporarily trapped by its neighbors before escaping.  The work highlights how even in a jam‑packed environment, subtle mechanical cues can dictate transport.  Because the full article is not available, the precise quantitative findings and any broader biological implications remain uncertain, but the snippet confirms the core concepts and the innovative use of MHz‑XPCS.
