The CODH paper's approach to studying gas channel gating was particularly elegant. The team faced a fundamental problem: how do you prove that a single amino acid residue is acting as a molecular gate for gas diffusion? A naive approach might just measure enzyme activity with and without the substrate, but that wouldn't distinguish between effects on binding versus effects on diffusion through the channel. Instead, they used a clever combination of site-directed mutagenesis at position 563, creating variants with different amino acid properties — small hydrophobic residues, polar residues, and charged residues. The key insight was that if Ile563 truly acts as a gate, then substitutions should affect both substrate access and inhibitor access in predictable ways based on the size and polarity of the replacement residue.

What made this particularly convincing was their use of multiple orthogonal measurements. They didn't just measure overall enzyme activity; they determined the Michaelis constant for CO (KMCO) for each variant, which directly reports on substrate binding affinity. They also tested oxygen sensitivity, since O2 is a competitive inhibitor that must also traverse the same channel. The pattern was striking: small hydrophobic substitutions like valine maintained near-wild-type function, while polar or charged substitutions dramatically increased KMCO and oxygen sensitivity. This suggests the channel's hydrophobic nature is crucial for selective gas transport.

The most compelling control was their use of a double mutant where they restored hydrophobicity at position 563 while changing other channel residues. This rescued function, proving it was specifically the loss of hydrophobicity at the gate, not just any mutation, that caused the defect. For anyone designing proteins with buried active sites, this approach of systematic mutagenesis combined with both substrate and inhibitor testing provides a template for dissecting how residues control molecular access through protein channels.
