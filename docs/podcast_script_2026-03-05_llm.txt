A massive observational study from the US Department of Veterans Affairs analyzed electronic health records of over 600,000 military veterans with type 2 diabetes. Researchers compared people prescribed GLP-1 drugs like semaglutide and liraglutide to a control group taking SGLT-2 inhibitors, another class of diabetes medication. Both groups were followed for up to three years.

The results were striking. Among people with no prior history of addiction, those taking GLP-1 drugs had a significantly lower risk of developing new substance-use disorders. The risk reduction was 18% for alcohol, 14% for cannabis, 20% for cocaine, 20% for nicotine, and 25% for opioids. For people already struggling with addiction, GLP-1 users had a 50% lower risk of dying from substance abuse compared to the control group.

These findings suggest GLP-1 medications may have broader therapeutic effects beyond weight loss and blood sugar control. The drugs work by mimicking a hormone that regulates appetite and reward pathways in the brain, which could explain their impact on addictive behaviors. However, this was an observational study, not a randomized controlled trial, so it cannot prove causation. Other factors could contribute to the observed differences.

The available text does not provide details on potential mechanisms or side effects specific to addiction treatment. It also does not specify which GLP-1 drugs were most effective or whether the benefits extend to people without diabetes. Despite these limitations, the large sample size and robust data from the VA database make these findings compelling. If confirmed in clinical trials, GLP-1 medications could become a valuable tool in addressing the global burden of substance-use disorders.

BioChemInsight is an open-source automated pipeline designed to extract chemical structures and their associated bioactivity data directly from patent documents. The system addresses a major bottleneck in drug discovery: the manual, time-intensive process of linking molecular structures to their biological activity profiles in patent literature.

The pipeline integrates four key components. First, it uses DECIMER Segmentation combined with MolNexTR for chemical structure recognition from images embedded in patents. Second, GLM-4.5V associates compound identifiers—such as chemical names and CAS registry numbers—with the recognized structures. Third, PaddleOCR handles text extraction from the patent documents. Finally, GLM-4.6 extracts bioactivity data and normalizes units across different reporting formats.

The system was evaluated on 181 patents covering 15 therapeutic targets. Across three core tasks—chemical structure recognition, bioactivity data extraction, and compound identifier association—it achieved an average accuracy above 90%. This performance marks a significant improvement over existing optical chemical structure recognition tools, which typically cannot autonomously link structures to their activity data.

One of the most striking findings is that the chemical space represented in patents is largely complementary to what's available in public databases like ChEMBL. This means BioChemInsight can uncover novel compound-target interactions that would otherwise remain hidden in the patent literature. The system also dramatically reduces data preprocessing time—from weeks to hours—enabling researchers to scale up their analyses.

By automating the extraction of structured chemical and bioactivity data from patents, BioChemInsight expands the landscape of explorable compound-target interactions. This enrichment is particularly valuable for quantitative structure-activity relationship modeling and targeted screening efforts. The open-source nature of the pipeline means it can be adapted and extended by the broader research community, potentially accelerating drug discovery workflows across multiple therapeutic areas.

Precision oncology is stuck in a frustrating bind. We have mountains of genomic data from cancer patients, but when it comes to knowing how a specific tumor will respond to a particular drug, we're often working with tiny sample sizes. This is the "small-N, large-P paradox" that plagues drug response prediction. Deep learning models can achieve impressive accuracy, but they operate as black boxes, offering predictions without explaining the biological mechanisms behind them.

A new framework called Contextual Invertible World Models aims to break this deadlock by combining machine learning with symbolic reasoning in what the authors call a "neuro-symbolic agentic framework." The system was trained on the Sanger GDSC dataset, which contains drug response data for 83 colorectal cancer cell lines.

Here's how it works: The framework has two main components. First, a predictive World Model that learns the complex relationships between genomic features and drug responses. Second, an LLM-based reasoning layer that can perform what the authors call "Inverse Reasoning" - essentially working backwards from a desired outcome to determine what genomic changes would produce it.

The key innovation is this ability to simulate specific genomic edits and predict their causal effects. For example, the system can model what would happen if you repaired a mutated APC gene or restored function to a broken TP53 tumor suppressor. This is essentially performing in silico CRISPR experiments to understand drug response mechanisms.

The framework explicitly incorporates clinical context, particularly Microsatellite Instability status, which is crucial for colorectal cancer treatment decisions. When tested, the system achieved a predictive correlation of r=0.504 on the GDSC dataset. More importantly, when it predicted how specific genomic edits would alter drug sensitivity, these predictions were validated against human clinical data with statistical significance (p=0.023).

This approach offers something current deep learning models cannot: transparency and causal understanding. Instead of just predicting that Drug X will work for Patient Y, it can explain why - perhaps because repairing Gene Z would restore drug sensitivity. This mechanistic insight could guide both treatment selection and the development of new therapeutic strategies.

The framework represents a shift from purely predictive models to explanatory ones, potentially transforming how we approach precision oncology by making the "why" as important as the "what" in drug response prediction.

Designing novel proteins with specific functions is one of the most challenging problems in biotechnology. The sequence space is enormous—even a modest protein of just 100 amino acids has 20^100 possible sequences, more than the number of atoms in the observable universe. Traditional methods of exploring this space through random mutation and selection are painfully slow and inefficient.

A new framework called BoGA, short for Bayesian Optimization Genetic Algorithm, tackles this problem by combining two powerful computational approaches. The genetic algorithm component generates new protein sequences by mimicking biological evolution—taking promising sequences and creating variations through mutation and recombination. The Bayesian optimization component then acts as a smart guide, using a surrogate model to predict which sequences are most likely to have the desired function.

Here's how it works: instead of blindly testing random sequences, BoGA uses the surrogate model to score potential candidates. It focuses experimental effort on sequences predicted to perform well, while also exploring new areas of sequence space that might contain better solutions. This creates a feedback loop where each experimental result improves the model's predictions, making the search increasingly efficient.

The framework was tested on both standard benchmark problems and a real-world challenge: designing peptides that bind to pneumolysin, a virulence factor from the bacterium that causes pneumonia. In the pneumolysin case, BoGA accelerated the discovery of high-confidence binder candidates compared to traditional approaches.

What makes this particularly powerful is the data efficiency. Because the method uses predictions to guide experiments, it can find good solutions with far fewer actual protein designs and tests than would be needed with brute-force approaches. This could dramatically speed up the development of new protein-based therapeutics, enzymes for industrial processes, and other biologics.

The available text does not provide details on the specific architecture of the surrogate model or the exact genetic algorithm operators used. It also doesn't discuss computational requirements or how the method scales with protein size.

The Rep protein from Vientovirus FB, a member of the Redondovirus genus, has been structurally characterized in multiple oligomeric states, revealing how nucleotide binding drives conformational changes critical for CRESS-DNA virus replication. This work presents the first high-resolution structures of a Redondovirus Rep protein, specifically the hexameric and dodecameric forms, which were captured using cryo-electron microscopy.

The protein purification was optimized under low-salt conditions—300 millimolar NaCl—which proved essential for stabilizing the oligomeric assemblies. To characterize these states, the researchers employed a suite of biophysical techniques: sedimentation velocity analytical ultracentrifugation, size-exclusion chromatography coupled with multi-angle light scattering, mass photometry, and synchrotron small-angle X-ray scattering. These methods collectively confirmed that fbRep exists as pentamers, hexamers, and dodecamers in solution.

The cryo-EM structures reveal that nucleotide binding induces allosteric changes in the ATPase domains of the protein. These conformational shifts are not merely structural curiosities—they directly link the energy state of the protein to its functional capacity in viral DNA replication. In CRESS-DNA viruses, Rep proteins perform three critical tasks: they nick the viral DNA to expose a 3' end for polymerase extension, provide NTP-dependent helicase activity to unwind DNA, and join nicked ends to form circular viral genomes. The structural data now provide a mechanistic framework for how these activities are coordinated.

What makes this work particularly significant is that it bridges the gap between the oligomeric state of the protein and its enzymatic function. The nucleotide-dependent conformational changes observed in the hexameric and dodecameric forms suggest a model where the assembly state and nucleotide occupancy together regulate the protein's ability to process viral DNA. This insight is crucial for understanding the replication strategies of CRESS-DNA viruses, a diverse group that includes pathogens of plants, animals, and humans.

The available text does not provide details on the specific nucleotide-bound states captured in the cryo-EM structures, nor does it specify the resolution of the structures. However, the combination of structural and biophysical data offers a robust foundation for future studies aimed at dissecting the precise molecular mechanisms of Rep-mediated replication.
