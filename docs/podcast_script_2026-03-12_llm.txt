A new guide from Asimov Press walks through the process of creating de novo antibodies—meaning antibodies built entirely from the ground up, without relying on existing immune responses.

The process starts with understanding the basic structure of an antibody. These proteins have a Y-shaped architecture, with two identical heavy chains and two identical light chains. The tips of the Y, called the variable regions, are where the magic happens—they bind to specific targets, like viruses or cancer cells. Designing a new antibody means engineering these variable regions to recognize a chosen target.

The first step is target selection. You need a clear idea of what you want the antibody to bind to—this could be a protein on a virus, a marker on a cancer cell, or any other molecule of interest. Once the target is chosen, the next challenge is designing the binding site. This involves predicting which amino acid sequences will fold into a structure that can snugly fit and bind to the target.

Computational tools play a big role here. Scientists use algorithms to model how different sequences might fold and interact with the target. The goal is to find a sequence that not only binds tightly but also remains stable and doesn't clump together or misfold. This is where things get tricky—small changes in sequence can have big effects on how the antibody behaves.

After designing the sequence, the next step is to actually make the antibody. This usually means synthesizing the DNA that encodes the antibody and putting it into cells—often bacteria or yeast—that can produce the protein. Once produced, the antibody needs to be tested for binding, stability, and any unwanted interactions.

The guide emphasizes that antibody design is an iterative process. Rarely does the first design work perfectly. Scientists often have to tweak the sequence, rerun simulations, and test again. It's a cycle of design, build, and test—much like engineering any complex system.

One of the key takeaways is that while the tools and knowledge for antibody design have advanced rapidly, it's still as much art as science. Predicting how a protein will fold and behave in the body is incredibly complex, and even small surprises can derail a design. But with each iteration, the process gets more refined, and the success rate improves.

In summary, designing antibodies from scratch involves selecting a target, using computational tools to predict and engineer binding sites, producing the protein in cells, and rigorously testing the results. It's a meticulous, iterative process that blends biology, computation, and engineering—pushing the boundaries of what we can create in the lab.
